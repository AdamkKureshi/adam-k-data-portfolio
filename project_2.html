The Dataset

Our dataset [Download the dataset] contains various factors that could influence the occurrence of a stroke. It includes a unique identifier for each record, as well as demographic and health-related attributes such as gender, age, and age group, which classifies individuals into categories like Teen, Adult, and Middle-Aged Adult. Health factors are captured through variables such as hypertension (indicating whether the individual has high blood pressure), heart disease (whether the individual has a history of heart disease), and diabetes category. Other variables include marital status, work type, smoking status, and body mass index (BMI), which is further categorized into ranges like Healthy Range, Overweight, and Obesity. The dataset also records stroke occurrence (whether the individual has had a stroke), as well as glucose levels, which may be linked to diabetes.

The categorization of age data plays a crucial role in analyzing and imputing missing data. By dividing age into meaningful categories such as Infant, Child, Adolescent, Adult, and Senior, we can gain more insights into the demographic structure of the dataset. The formula utilized assigns individuals to these categories based on their age, ensuring that each record falls into the appropriate group. This is important for a few reasons: it allows us to apply more accurate imputation techniques for missing values, as we can infer missing data based on the trends and patterns within each age group. Moreover, age categories help in identifying relationships between age and other health-related variables such as hypertension, heart disease, and glucose levels, thus enabling more targeted data analysis and decision-making. This approach enhances the quality of the dataset and facilitates better-informed conclusions in further analyses.

The distribution of these variables reveals a wide age range, with individuals spanning from teens to elderly adults, suggesting a varied population. Gender representation includes both males and females, and further analysis could explore whether there are gender-based differences in stroke occurrences. Hypertension and heart disease are binary indicators (yes or no), and both might show a direct correlation with stroke occurrences. Stroke is the target variable, and its distribution indicates a minority of individuals have had a stroke. BMI varies across individuals, with some in the healthy range and others categorized as overweight or obese, which could potentially correlate with stroke risk. This dataset provides valuable insight into the factors that may contribute to stroke occurrence and can be used for deeper analysis into the relationship between these health variables and stroke.

The dataset shows a relatively low prevalence of hypertension, with only 9.75% of individuals affected, while 5.4% have heart disease. Initial Steps

Handling missing values is a crucial step in preparing data for analysis because missing data can skew results, introduce bias, or limit the reliability of insights. The first step is to determine the nature of the missing values to choose an appropriate strategy. Missing data can occur for several reasons: systematic missingness, where data is absent due to design constraints (e.g., certain questions not applicable to specific groups); random missingness, where values are missing by chance, making imputation feasible; or not applicable, where a data field does not apply to certain entries (e.g., BMI for very young individuals). Understanding why data is missing helps to define whether values should be imputed, removed, or left untouched.

For instance, in the dataset used here, the bmi column has 201 missing values. One option is to remove rows where bmi is missing. This approach works well if the variable is not central to the analysis or if the missing data constitutes a small proportion of the dataset (e.g., less than 5–10%). In tools like Excel or Google Sheets, you can filter and exclude rows with blank values in the bmi column. However, filtering out rows is not always ideal, especially if the dataset is already small or if the missing values are significant.

What to do with missing data

An alternative is imputation, which involves replacing missing values with estimates. The simplest imputation methods are mean or median imputation, where missing values are filled with the average or median of the column. This works well when the data is normally distributed or has a uniform spread. In spreadsheets like Excel, this can be done using formulas like =AVERAGE(range) or =MEDIAN(range) and applying the result to missing cells. A more tailored approach is category-based imputation, where missing values are replaced with the mean or median of similar groups, such as by Age Group or gender. This accounts for potential variations across categories.

However, when columns have a very high percentage of missing values (e.g., over 99%), it is often best to drop them entirely, as they provide little value to the analysis. For example, columns like Unnamed: 18, BMI, and Glucose_lvl in this dataset have too many missing values to be useful. In Excel, these can be deleted manually - however, for our dataset we do not need to delete any columns.

To effectively manage missing data, it is essential to highlight and analyze where and why the gaps exist. Identifying patterns in missing values can offer valuable insights into the data collection process and inform the best approach for addressing them. For instance, missing BMI values might be more prevalent in specific age groups, genders, or other categories. Spotting such trends can guide decisions about whether imputation should consider these factors. Spreadsheet packages make it simple to visualize missing data patterns. In Excel, conditional formatting can be applied using a formula like =ISBLANK(cell) to highlight missing cells, allowing you to quickly assess their distribution. By analyzing these patterns, you can decide if missing data is random, systematic, or potentially linked to an underlying issue.

Once you've decided on a method to handle missing values, it's important to document the rationale behind your choice. This documentation adds credibility to your analysis and demonstrates your understanding of the dataset. Clearly explain why you selected a particular approach, such as mean imputation for simplicity. To show the impact of your decision, include before-and-after statistics or visualizations that highlight changes in the dataset, such as differences in column averages or distributions. This not only makes your process transparent but also serves as a valuable learning resource for others reviewing your work.

After making changes to address missing data, it’s crucial to validate the results to ensure they align with your analytical goals. Check that the dataset still retains enough rows and columns to support meaningful analysis and that no key insights have been lost. Additionally, review the imputed values to confirm they are realistic and consistent with the overall data distribution. For example, ensure that imputed BMI values fall within a plausible range for the respective age groups and genders. By systematically highlighting, documenting, and validating missing data handling, you ensure the integrity of your analysis and showcase your meticulous approach to data preparation.

Analyze: Extracting Insights from Cleaned Data in Excel/Google Sheets

nce the data is cleaned and transformed, the analysis phase is where you begin to uncover meaningful insights. Using Excel or Google Sheets, you can start with descriptive analysis, which involves calculating summary statistics such as averages, medians, and percentages. For instance, you can create pivot tables to count stroke occurrences across different Age Groups, BMI Categories, or smoking_status. To make the data more interpretable, leverage visualizations like bar charts to compare stroke occurrences by age or gender, and pie charts to illustrate the proportion of smokers versus non-smokers among individuals who had strokes. These visuals can quickly communicate trends and distributions to an audience.

Beyond descriptive analysis, dive deeper into the dataset to uncover exploratory insights. For example, check whether higher BMI categories or older age groups correlate with increased stroke risk. Use scatter plots in Excel/Google Sheets to visualize relationships between numeric variables, such as avg_glucose_level and age, and their association with stroke. For more structured analysis, calculate correlations between numeric columns (e.g., age, bmi, avg_glucose_level) to assess their strength of association with stroke outcomes. In Google Sheets, you can use the =CORREL(array1, array2) formula to calculate these relationships directly, identifying key variables that might serve as predictors of stroke risk.

Share: Presenting Your Findings Effectively in Excel/Google Sheets

After analyzing the data, focus on presenting your findings in a visually appealing and portfolio-friendly manner. Google Sheets and Excel offer numerous options for creating polished charts and dashboards. Start by building interactive dashboards that allow viewers to explore the data dynamically. For example, use slicers or filters in pivot tables to let stakeholders focus on specific groups (e.g., males versus females or different age brackets). Incorporate visuals such as heatmaps that highlight stroke percentages across Age Groups and BMI Categories, or bar charts comparing stroke risk by work_type. Google Sheets' built-in charting tools are sufficient for creating clean visuals, but you can also link the data to Google Data Studio for more interactive dashboards.

In addition to charts, focus on data storytelling by formatting your spreadsheets for clarity and impact. Use clear labels, descriptive titles, and concise annotations to explain key visuals. For instance, under a chart showing stroke risk by age group, add a note: “Stroke risk increases sharply after age 60, especially among individuals with hypertension.” Employ conditional formatting to highlight critical insights, such as cells where glucose levels exceed a risk threshold. The goal is to create a seamless narrative from the raw data to actionable insights, making it easy for others to understand the results without extensive explanations.
